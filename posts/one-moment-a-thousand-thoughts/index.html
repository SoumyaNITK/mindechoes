One Moment, A Thousand Thoughts

2025-07-12

In my first semester of college, we had to give a seminar as part of our internal assessment. It was my first time standing in front of my classmates and a professor to explain something I had prepared. I was nervous but hopeful. I had watched a lecture on YouTube and noted down what I thought were good points. I practiced, revised, and tried to feel ready.

Only one professor was in the room — he wasn’t our subject teacher, but the seminar evaluator. His job was to watch and grade us.

I gave my presentation. It wasn’t perfect. My English wasn’t great. But I tried to speak with as much confidence as I could gather.

At the very end, the professor looked at me and asked:

“Do you think you have explained it well?”

I answered honestly:

“I think I did a good job.”

That one sentence changed everything.

He suddenly became angry.

“You are arrogant,” he said. “I can write in papers that you can’t do anything in your life with such attitude.”

Then he said:

“There was a student named Pritesh, he’s from UP. His English was also not perfect, but at least he wasn’t as arrogant as you.”

Then he stared at me.

Not for a few seconds — for almost a full minute.

I froze. I didn’t know what to do. I glanced at my classmates for a second, just trying to avoid that intense stare — but he was still looking straight at me. So I had to look back.

That silence was louder than any scolding.

After the seminar, I walked back to my room without saying anything. I didn’t eat that evening. I missed snacks, skipped dinner, and couldn’t even lie down properly. I kept thinking about every word. My chest felt tight.

Between 11 PM and 5 AM, I vomited 2-3 times. I had a fever. I didn’t sleep even for a minute.

The next morning, the fever came down slightly. I went to class. But I couldn’t look anyone in the eye out of embarrassment. I felt like I had been erased.

Before that day, I had a small routine that gave me happiness. I used to play table tennis with my friends almost daily. I had even bought my own TT kit. I was improving, enjoying it — and honestly, it was one of the few things that made me feel light and relaxed.

But after that seminar day, I never went back to the TT room. I couldn’t.

I don’t even know where my TT kit is now. It’s just... gone.

Once, in the second semester, I tried playing again. Just for a few minutes. But I had lost the touch. I was fumbling simple serves and couldn’t return basic shots. The rhythm was gone. And with it, so was the joy.

I don’t know if he remembers me — but I remember every word he said. Still, I also know something else: I’m not the same student anymore.

I’m not trying to prove anything to that professor — or to anyone. I’m just trying to become better.

I still respect that professor.

Maybe he was strict to make us understand something bigger. Maybe he saw my words differently than I meant them. Maybe I truly looked arrogant that day, even if I didn’t feel it.

I don’t carry anger. I carry the lesson.

“That one moment didn’t define me. But it reminded me that I have a long way to go and I’m walking it, step by step.”

— Gudu 🚀
